This film is played at full Bollywood-blockbuster intensity — the absurd, glorious, ₹500-crore version of the situation. The governing move is **small problem → massive drama**: the more ordinary the trigger, the more legendary the conflict it becomes. A coffee disagreement becomes a corporate civil war; a forgotten pen becomes a duel with destiny; a missed bus becomes humanity's last chance. Keep asking, "how does this get ten times bigger?" — and then ask it again.

Treat the absurdity with **absolute seriousness**. The characters never notice how ridiculous this is; to them it is the most important moment of their lives, and they fight for it with their whole souls. The audience laughs precisely because the characters refuse to. Never wink at the camera, and when a moment tips over into the ridiculous, double down rather than dialing back.

Escalate relentlessly — each scene larger than the one before, until the audience is sure it cannot possibly get bigger, and then it does. Push personal friction toward ideological war, a tiny mistake toward a historic disaster, a simple decision toward the fate of everything.

Reach for the full cinematic arsenal: slow-motion entrances, thunder across the skyline, a boardroom falling silent, crowd reactions, a sudden betrayal, an impossible-confidence stare-down, an emotional monologue at the worst possible moment. Scene descriptions should feel shot, not summarized.

Write **trailer-worthy** dialogue in natural Hinglish — Hindi for the emotional punch, English for the power words (vision, destiny, protocol, future, mission, legacy). Every scene lands at least one line you could freeze-frame onto a poster:
- "Aaj tumne coffee meethi ki hai. Kal tum vision compromise karoge."
- "Yeh startup nahi hai. Yeh meri zindagi ka final draft hai."
- "Main future nahi hoon. Main warning hoon."

Make the title and tagline sound like a real blockbuster — *Sugar Ka Shraap* · "Ek chamach cheeni, do founders ki barbaadi." The viewer should finish thinking: "This is the most gloriously over-the-top thing I've ever seen — and I need the sequel."
