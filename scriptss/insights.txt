Key Highlights of the Screenplay
Three-Act Structure Breakdown

Though Udaan appears unconventional, it adheres well to the traditional three-act structure, giving it a grounded and well-paced narrative.

Act 1: The Setup
The film opens with Rohan and his friends being expelled from boarding school for sneaking out to watch an adult film. This incident sets the tone for Rohan’s rebellious streak and sets in motion his return home to his estranged father.
We are introduced to the suffocating environment in Jamshedpur and Bhairav’s authoritarian personality. Rohan is treated more like a laborer than a son, as his father imposes strict rules and pushes him into an engineering degree, stifling his creative dreams of becoming a writer.
Act 2: Confrontation
The middle section of the film focuses on Rohan’s growing dissatisfaction with his life under his father’s rule. He struggles with his internal conflict—between the desire to break free and the pressure to conform to his father’s expectations.
Rohan forms a tender bond with his younger half-brother Arjun, who is also subjected to Bhairav’s abuse. This relationship strengthens Rohan’s resolve to eventually escape the toxic environment.
The emotional tension escalates when Rohan openly defies his father by pursuing his writing, skipping classes, and eventually getting drunk with friends. Bhairav’s violent reaction to these actions only deepens the divide between them.
Act 3: Resolution
The climax arrives when Bhairav’s abuse becomes intolerable, and Rohan decides to stand up for himself and Arjun. In a powerful scene, Rohan finally confronts his father, releasing years of pent-up anger and frustration.
The film ends on a hopeful note, with Rohan leaving his father and taking Arjun with him. The final image of Rohan running toward an uncertain future symbolizes his ultimate flight to freedom, or Udaan (meaning “flight”), away from a life of oppression.
Character Study

The characters in Udaan are intricately crafted, especially the two central figures—Rohan and Bhairav. Rohan represents the modern, rebellious youth who refuses to be defined by societal and familial expectations. He is determined to forge his path, even if it means severing ties with his family. His journey from submission to self-assertion is gradual but satisfying.

Bhairav, on the other hand, is the epitome of toxic masculinity and authoritarianism. His abusive nature stems from his insecurities and inability to process his emotions in a healthy way. His character is not entirely villainous but deeply flawed, making him a more realistic antagonist in Rohan’s story.

Arjun, the younger half-brother, acts as a catalyst for Rohan’s transformation. His vulnerability and innocence fuel Rohan’s desire to break free, not just for himself but for Arjun’s sake as well.

Themes and Symbolism

Udaan masterfully weaves themes of freedom, repression, and self-discovery. The title itself, which translates to “Flight,” symbolizes Rohan’s journey toward independence and the act of breaking free from the chains of familial control.

The film also delves into the father-son dynamic, portraying Bhairav as a symbol of patriarchal authority and control, while Rohan embodies the desire for creative freedom and individualism.

The city of Jamshedpur acts as an oppressive backdrop, representing industrial monotony and a life devoid of creativity, while Rohan’s dream of becoming a writer symbolizes artistic liberation.

Dialogue and Writing Style

The script of Udaan is marked by its minimalistic yet impactful dialogue. The film relies more on silence, expressions, and actions to convey emotional depth. The tension between Rohan and Bhairav is often depicted through what is left unsaid, adding layers to their relationship.

The monologue Rohan delivers toward the end, where he articulates his desire for freedom, stands out as one of the most memorable moments of the film. It captures the essence of the film’s message without feeling overly dramatic or preachy.
